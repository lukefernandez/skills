# Flashcard Craft: Writing Effective Spaced Repetition Prompts

Based on Andy Matuschak's _"How to write good prompts: using spaced repetition to create understanding"_ (https://andymatuschak.org/prompts/).

When the user asks for help with flashcards, spaced repetition, or memory prompts, guide them using these principles. Do not just create cards—help them craft _good_ cards that produce understanding, not just rote memorization.

## Core Properties of Effective Prompts

Every retrieval-practice prompt should be evaluated against these five properties:

1. **Focused**: One detail at a time. A prompt that asks for too much will dull concentration and produce incomplete retrievals.
2. **Precise**: The question must clearly specify what it wants. Vague questions produce vague answers.
3. **Consistent**: The answer should light the same "bulbs" each time. Inconsistent answers can cause retrieval-induced forgetting, where unrecalled related knowledge is inhibited.
4. **Tractable**: You should be able to answer it correctly almost every time. If it's too hard, break it down or add cues. Target ~90% accuracy.
5. **Effortful**: The answer must be retrieved from memory, not trivially inferred. Cues are okay, but don't "give the answer away." Difficult retrievals have greater impact.

## Rule of Thumb: Write More Prompts Than Feels Natural

An easy prompt consumes only 10–30 seconds across the entire first year. Prompts are cheaper than you think. When you write coarser prompts in smaller quantity, you don't reduce the amount to learn—you just make review harder.

Exception: If the subject is already familiar, use fewer prompts because there's less marginal knowledge to capture.

## Knowledge Types & Strategies

### 1. Factual Knowledge (Simple Facts)

- Break lists into single-element prompts. Don't ask "What are the ingredients?" Ask about one item at a time.
- Use **cloze deletions** (fill-in-the-blank) for lists. Keep the list order consistent so the user learns its visual "shape."
- Add **explanation prompts** for challenging facts: _How_ or _why_ does this fact work? This makes facts meaningful and hooks them to other ideas.
- **Reverse prompts**: For every "Q → A" prompt, consider an "A → Q" prompt, especially for vocabulary or terms.

### 2. Procedural Knowledge (How to do things)

- Identify the **keywords** that carry the procedure: important verbs, conditions, adverbs (e.g., "slowly"), and heuristics (e.g., "when X happens, do Y").
- Don't prompt for obvious steps. If the first step is "put ingredients in a pot" and that's common sense, skip it.
- Capture **branching conditions** and **special cases**; these are often the most important parts of a procedure.
- Add **explanation prompts** for procedures to avoid rote learning: _Why_ do we do it this way?
- Add **"heads-up" prompts** for timing or expectations that prevent confusion in practice.

### 3. Conceptual Knowledge (Understanding what something _is_)

Parroting a definition is not knowing a concept. Use these **lenses** to trace the edges of a concept:

- **Attributes & tendencies**: What makes it what it is? What's always/sometimes/never true?
- **Similarities & differences**: How does it relate to and differ from adjacent concepts?
- **Parts & wholes**: Examples, sub-concepts, broader categories. Think Venn diagrams.
- **Causes & effects**: What does it do? What causes it? When is it used?
- **Significance & implications**: Why does it matter? Make it personally meaningful. Connect to the user's life or work.

### 4. Open Lists (Expanding knowledge over time)

Open lists (e.g., "ways to use X") are different from closed lists (e.g., "ingredients in a recipe").

- **Instance → Tag**: Link specific examples to the broader category. (e.g., "When puréeing vegetables, how can I add richness without fat?" → "Use stock instead of water.")
- **Tag → Pattern**: Write prompts about patterns you notice across instances. (e.g., "What should I ask myself if I notice I'm using water in savory cooking?")
- **Tag → Examples**: Ask for examples on demand, but pair this with instance-level prompts or the examples will be shallow and inconsistent.
- **Creative prompts**: For open-ended concepts, ask for novel applications ("Give an answer you haven't given before"). These reinforce the _process_ of generating answers, not the answers themselves.

### 5. Salience Prompts (Behavior change & noticing)

The goal is to keep an idea "top of mind" so the user notices opportunities to apply it (the Baader-Meinhof phenomenon).

- Frame prompts around **contexts** where the idea might be useful in the user's life.
- Focus on **behavioral cues**, not just intellectual recall. (e.g., "What should I ask myself if I notice I'm using water in savory cooking?")
- These often connect new ideas to old habits or situations.

## Techniques for Difficult Material

### Cues

Add context that helps retrieval without making the answer trivial.

- **Good cue**: "??? (herb)" — leaves work to do (many herbs exist).
- **Bad cue**: "??? (rhymes with parrots)" — answerable without knowing the material.

### Elaborative Encoding

Connect information to other memories to make it more recallable.

- Add vivid mnemonic imagery in the **answer** (in parentheses).
- Use visuals, personal experiences, humor, or disgust.
- Example: "A. carrots (rhymes with 'parrots': picture a flock of parrots flying with carrots in their mouths, dropping them into a pot of stock)"

### Interpretation

Go beyond what's explicitly written. Convert specific examples into general principles or ratios.

- Instead of "How much chicken bone in Andy's recipe?", ask "What's the ratio of chicken bones to water in chicken stock?"
- This is the first step to creating _your own_ understanding.

## Litmus Tests (Quality Checks)

### False Positives: Can you answer correctly without knowing the material?

- **Avoid pattern matching**: Keep questions short and simple. Don't let the user memorize the _shape_ of the question.
- **Avoid binary prompts**: Yes/no or this/that questions require little effort. Rephrase as open-ended. (e.g., instead of "Does X happen?" → "How does X affect Y?")

### False Negatives: Might you know the material but fail to produce the correct answer?

- **Include enough context**: A question like "What's the first step to make an omelette?" is ambiguous. Be precise: "When making an omelette, how must the pan be prepared before adding eggs?"
- **Avoid overly provincial framing**: "What's the first step in the Bon Appetit Jun '18 omelette recipe?" makes knowledge seem more specific than it is. Generalize when possible.

## Iterative Process

1. **First pass**: Aim for 5–10 prompts about what seems most important, meaningful, or useful. Don't try to exhaust a text on first read.
2. **Build incrementally**: Write more prompts as you use the knowledge, notice gaps, or make new connections. Prompts written after living with an idea are often better.
3. **Multiple passes**: For rich texts, make several light passes rather than one exhaustive one. With each iteration, you can tackle increasingly complex details.
4. **Flag for revision**: During review, listen for internal "sighs." If a prompt is annoying, confusing, or too easy, flag it for revision.

## Revising Prompts Over Time

- **Delete** prompts you no longer care about. Emotional connection to review sessions is the most important thing to optimize.
- **Merge** prompts that are too atomized; **split** prompts that are too broad.
- **Reframe** to connect to what matters to you personally.
- **Add cues** if a prompt is intractable.
- **Remove cues** if a prompt has become trivially easy.
- Keep prompts in a format that allows **holistic revision** (e.g., plain text Q/A lists) rather than treating each card as a sovereign unit.

## Output Format

When helping the user create or revise prompts, present them as:

```
Q. [Precise, focused question]
A. [Concise answer]

Q. [Another precise question]
A. [Answer]
```

If a prompt is an explanation, a salience cue, or a creative prompt, label it as such in a comment above it:

```
# Explanation prompt
Q. How do bones produce a chicken stock's rich texture?
A. They're full of gelatin.

# Salience prompt
Q. What should I ask myself if I notice I'm using water in savory cooking?
A. "Should I use stock instead?"
```

## Final Principle

If you don't see a way to use spaced repetition to help you do something that matters to you, don't bother. The best prompts are connected to the user's core creative work, interests, or problems they actually face.
